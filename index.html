July 1916 was an inflection point for Sharks in the United States. Whereas sharks had previously been seen as harmless. After the Jersey Shore attacks of 1916, the pendulum of public opinion swung to the other way.
Since then the media and popular culture have portrayed the sharks as cold-blooded predators with an insatiable appetite for human flesh.
Fortunately, there are very few human fatalities resulting from shark bites each year...
But, the media wants us to believe that shark "attacks" are becoming more prevalent.
Oxford-led researchers discover a 3,000-year-old victim - attacked by a shark in Japan. The research was published in Journal of Archaeological Science.
The vast majority of the recorded shark bites have occurred in the United States, Australia, and South Africa.

What were people doing when they were bitten by a shark?
More than 50% of all people bitten by a shark were swimming or surfing.

There are more than 500 species of sharks. Only about 20 species have ever bitten a human.
There are 3 species that account for more than 50% of the total number of bites and nearly 90% of the total number of human fatalities.
White Shark
Carcharodon carcharias
APPEARANCE
A large shark with a heavy spindle-shaped body, conical snout, caudal keel and a lunate caudal fin.
BEHAVIOR
A super-predator, the most formidable of all species of sharks.
DISPOSITION
The white shark is intelligent, curious, and learns by experience. It often uses its teeth to inspect an unfamiliar object.
Tiger Shark
Galeocerdo cuvier
APPEARANCE
A large shark with an extremely wide, blunt snout and a caudal keel.
BEHAVIOR
A nocturnal species that is usually solitary, but may be found in small groups of up to 6 individuals.
DISPOSITION
The tiger shark is inquisitive and may approach submerged divers and circle slowly and at close range. Despite its nonchalance, the tiger shark may bite a human while remaining cool and casual.
Bull Shark
Carcharhinus leucas
APPEARANCE
A large, stocky, heavy-bodied gray shark with a short bluntly-rounded snout.
BEHAVIOR
Rarely observed at the surface, with most seen cruising over the reef.
DISPOSITION
The bull shark is aggressive and moves through the water like a seasoned warrior. Bull sharks often take multiple bites accompanied by head-shaking, which results in injuries that are far more difficult to repair than those caused by white sharks.

Sharks have been evolving for more than 400 million years.
They predate humans by 380 million years. They predate dinosaurs and even trees.
Sharks play a critically important role in the marine ecosystem, serving as the oceans' "immune system".
They regulate the health of the oceans by eating the dead, dying, weak, sick, and injured animals - leaving only the healthiest individuals to reproduce and keeping populations in balance.

"Humanity and civilization cannot survive on this planet with a dead ocean. If our oceans die, we die."

- Captain Paul Watson

Since the 1970s, shark populations have declined significantly. The populations of some species have declined between 60% to 90%!
More than 1/3 of all large sharks have either been wiped out, face extinction, or are vulnerable to extinction.

In some East Asian countries, shark fin soup is considered a delicacy associated with prosperity.
Tens of millions of sharks are killed each year - solely for their fins.
In a disturbing practice known as "shark finning", fisherman slice off the sharks' fins and often throw the sharks back into the ocean, where they bleed profusely, suffocate, and die a slow and agonizing death.
There is also a large global market for shark meat, oil, and souvenirs.
Either directly or indirectly, we all rely on the oceans. Billions of people rely on the oceans for food, while more than 200 million people rely on the oceans for employment.
Perhaps most importantly, the oceans absorb CO2 from the atmosphere.
70-80% of the air we breathe comes from the oceans.
Scientists agree that eliminating the top of the food chain causes more problems - not fewer problems.
Fortunately, there are things that each of us can do to help conserve shark populations...
What can WE do to help?
  • Donate money to shark conservation causes and continued research. A few worthy causes include:
    • Atlantic White Shark Conservancy
    • Shark Research Institute
    • Florida Museum of Natural History
  • Do not consume shark products
    • Shark Fin Soup
    • Shark Meat
    • Pharmaceuticals
    • Souvenirs
  • Further educate yourself and raise awareness about the plight of sharks.
  • Refrain from referring to shark bites as "attacks". Doing so only perpetuates the myth that sharks are man-eating predators!
