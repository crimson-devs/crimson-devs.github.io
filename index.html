In July 1916, five people were "attacked" by sharks while swimming along the coast of New Jersey.
Since then, the media and popular culture have unjustly characterized sharks as predators with an insatiable appetite for human flesh.
But, nothing could be further from the truth.
Let's redefine the relationship we have with sharks to one based on scientific data rather than limited an biased information.

Since the 1970s, shark populations have declined by a staggering 70%.
More than 1/3 of all large sharks have either been wiped out, face extinction, or are vulnerable to extinction.

One of the biggest risks facing sharks is bycatch - the unintentional capture of non-target species by fisherman.
Bycatch is responsible for an estimated 50 million shark deaths each year.
There is a large global market for shark meat, oil, and souvenirs.
In some East Asian countries, shark fin soup is considered a delicacy associated with prosperity.
Tens of millions of sharks are killed each year - solely for their fins.
In a disturbing practice known as "shark finning", fisherman slice off the sharks' fins and often throw the sharks back into the ocean, where they bleed profusely, suffocate, and die a slow and agonizing death.

Either directly or indirectly, we all rely on the oceans. Billions of people rely on the oceans for seafood, while more than 200 million people rely on the oceans for employment.
Perhaps most importantly, the oceans absorb CO2 from the atmosphere.
70-80% of the air we breathe comes from the oceans.
Scientists agree that eliminating the top of the food chain causes more problems - not fewer problems. Fortunately, there are things that each of us can do to help...

What can you do to help?
  • Donate money to shark conservation causes and continued research. A few worthy causes include:
    • Atlantic White Shark Conservancy
    • Shark Research Institute
    • Florida Museum of Natural History
  • Do not eat shark fin soup (and discourage businesses from selling it).
  • Do not consume shark meat.
  • Do not purchase souvenirs or pharmaceuticals made from shark body parts.
  • Further educate yourself and raise awareness about the plight of sharks.
  • Refrain from referring to encounters between sharks and humans as "attacks". Doing so only perpetuates the myth!
